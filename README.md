# cs50_final_project
# LIFE BOARD : Terminal Based Routine Planner
#### Video Demo:  https://youtu.be/Ox5Nm5n4mrY
#### Description: 
As a student, I often find myself forgetting assignment deadlines or realizing too late that I've spent hours on one subject while neglecting others. On top of that, with a limited monthly allowance, I often give in to little impulses like buying snacks or small things that aren't really necessary and then feel frustrated when I see how quickly my money disappears. I realized I needed something to help me stay on track, not just academically, but also financially, something that could guide me without being overwhelming. That's when I decided to build my own program, a personal assistant of sorts, to help me organize my tasks, track my study hours, and manage my spending all while giving me little nudges of motivation for my final project.

When I started coding, I debated a lot about which features to include. I wanted the program to be helpful without being too complicated. For example, in the task tracker, I wasn't sure whether I should display all dates I had entered or let the user type a specific one. Showing all dates seemed like it would require changes in multiple places I thought were finished. I decided to start simple and improve over time. There were moments when I spent hours, sometimes even days perfecting a single function, running the program over and over to see how it worked. It was frustrating, but every small success felt like a victory and pushed me to keep going.

At one point, I showed my program to a friend who's also a student. I asked him to use it as if he were a normal user, not a coder, and tell me what he thought. He pointed out that it would be helpful to have an option to delete a specific date's tasks instead of removing just one task at a time.  Implementing this feature was tough, I had to study Python's os module and figure out how to update the list of dates once a date was deleted. But making that change made the program feel more complete and user-friendly. I learned that good program isn't just about making it run, it's about thinking about the person who will use it.

The program became a mix of a task manager, study journal, and budget tracker. The task tracker helps me record assignments and deadlines, and even delete them when necessary. The study journal logs how much time I spend on each subject and gives short motivational messages and even let me store notes. The budget tracker tracks income and expenses so I can see where my money is going, which helps me feel more accountable. Writing down transactions gives me a sense of control, suddenly I can see how small impulses add up and how much I can save if I stay mindful.

Working on this project taught me a lot about discipline and patience. I learned that coding isn't just about writing lines that work, it’s about testing, rethinking, and improving. It’s about putting myself in the user’s shoes and designing something intuitive. I also learned that small touches, like encouraging messages or simple tables showing tasks and transactions, make a big difference in user experience.

Looking back, what I'm most proud of isn't just the program itself, but the process. Every function represents a habit I wanted to improve: staying organized, managing time better, and being mindful of money. Every time I log a study session, check off a task, or record an expense, I feel a sense of accomplishment. What started as an assignment became a journey of learning, reflection, and self-improvement.
